How to Reduce Image File Size for Email

"This file is too large to attach." Modern phone cameras produce photos of 5–12 MB each, and a handful of them blow straight past email limits. Here's how to shrink images so they send instantly — without anyone noticing a drop in quality.

Know your email's attachment limit

Each provider caps the total size of attachments per message. Stay comfortably under these and your email goes through every time.

ProviderAttachment limit
Gmail25 MB
Outlook / Outlook.com20 MB
Yahoo Mail25 MB
Apple Mail (iCloud)20 MB

These are totals for the whole message, so five 6 MB photos already exceed Gmail's limit. The fix is to make each image smaller before attaching.

Three levers that shrink a photo

File size comes from three things, and you can pull any of them:

  1. Dimensions. A 4000-pixel-wide photo has far more data than a 1500-pixel one. Most photos don't need to be larger than ~1600 px to look great on a screen.
  2. Quality (compression). Dropping JPG quality from 100% to around 80% can halve the file with no visible difference.
  3. Format. Converting to WebP often cuts size another 25–35% versus JPG.

Step by step: get a photo under the limit

  1. Resize first. Open the resizer and set the long edge to about 1600 px. This alone removes most of the bulk. See how to resize an image for details.
  2. Then compress. Lower the quality to roughly 80% and check the preview. Our compression guide explains how to find the sweet spot.
  3. Optionally convert. If the recipient can open WebP, convert JPG to WebP for an extra size cut.
  4. Check the result. A typical photo drops from ~8 MB to a few hundred kilobytes — small enough to attach a dozen of them.

Sending a whole batch

If you're emailing an entire event's worth of photos, process them together with the batch tool. It resizes and compresses many images in one pass, so you're not repeating the steps file by file.

A privacy note before you send

Photos carry hidden EXIF metadata, including the GPS location where they were taken. If you're emailing images to someone you don't fully trust with that information, strip it first with the EXIF remover — see our EXIF guide.

Frequently asked questions

How small should an emailed photo be?

For normal viewing on a screen, aim for under 1 MB per image. That keeps your total message well under any provider's limit and still looks sharp when opened.

Will compressing ruin the photo?

Not at sensible settings. Around 80% JPG quality is visually indistinguishable from the original for most photos while being dramatically smaller. Only heavy compression below ~60% starts to show artifacts.

Are my images uploaded when I shrink them here?

No. All resizing and compression happens inside your browser, so the photos never leave your device and nothing is stored on a server.
