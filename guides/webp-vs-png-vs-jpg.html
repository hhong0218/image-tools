WebP vs PNG vs JPG: Which Should You Use?

These three formats cover almost every image you'll ever save, and picking the right one is the single easiest way to balance quality against file size. Here's how they actually differ — and a one-line rule you can remember.

The short answer

Use WebP for the web whenever you can, JPG for maximum compatibility with photos, and PNG when you need crisp edges or transparency. That covers 95% of real situations. The rest of this guide explains why.

JPG — the universal photo format

JPG has been the default for photos for decades. It uses lossy compression with an adjustable quality setting, which makes photo files small while keeping them looking good. The trade-offs: it has no transparency, and its compression smears sharp edges — so text and line art look fuzzy. If something will be opened by an unknown, possibly ancient piece of software, JPG is the safest bet.

PNG — lossless and transparent

PNG is lossless, meaning the saved image is mathematically identical to the original. That makes it perfect for logos, icons, UI screenshots, and any graphic with hard edges or text, where JPG's smearing would be obvious. It also supports full alpha transparency. The cost is file size: a photo saved as PNG can be several times larger than the same photo as JPG, which is why you should avoid PNG for photographs.

WebP — the modern all-rounder

WebP, developed by Google, is the newest of the three and in most ways the best. It supports both lossy and lossless modes, handles transparency like PNG, and produces files roughly 25–35% smaller than JPG at the same visible quality. It even supports animation. Every current major browser supports it. The only reason not to use WebP is when a file has to be opened by old desktop software or a service that doesn't accept it yet.

How to choose, in practice

  • Photo for a website? WebP first; fall back to JPG if needed.
  • Photo for email or an upload form? JPG — it always works.
  • Logo, icon, or screenshot with text? PNG (or lossless WebP).
  • Need transparency? PNG or WebP — never JPG.
  • Want the smallest possible file? WebP, almost every time.

Frequently asked questions

Is WebP better than JPG?

For the web, almost always — WebP files are smaller at the same quality and support transparency. JPG only wins on raw compatibility with very old software.

Should I use PNG or JPG for photos?

JPG. PNG photos are needlessly huge because PNG is lossless; reserve PNG for graphics with sharp edges or transparency.

Does converting JPG to PNG improve quality?

No. Quality already lost to JPG compression can't be recovered; the PNG just preserves the (already compressed) result at a larger file size.
