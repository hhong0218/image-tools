How to Resize an Image Online

Maybe an upload form rejected your photo for being too big, or a profile picture needs to be exactly 400×400. Resizing changes the pixel dimensions of an image, and you can do it in seconds without installing anything or handing your photo to a server.

Resize vs. compress — they're not the same

People often say "resize" when they really mean "make the file smaller," but those are two different operations. Resizing changes the number of pixels — a 4000×3000 photo becomes, say, 1200×900. Compressing keeps the pixel dimensions the same but throws away data to shrink the file on disk. Resizing almost always reduces file size too, because fewer pixels means less to store, but the goal is different. If your only problem is a bloated file, see our guide to compressing without losing quality.

The fastest way: resize in your browser

You don't need Photoshop or a phone app. The resizer on our home page runs entirely in your browser — your image is processed locally and never uploaded.

  1. Open the ImageTools resizer and make sure the Resize tab is selected.
  2. Drag your photo onto the drop zone, or click to browse.
  3. Type a target width or height in pixels, or switch to a percentage like 50%.
  4. Keep Lock aspect ratio on so the image doesn't stretch (more on that below).
  5. Download the resized image. That's it.

Keep the aspect ratio (or your photo gets squished)

Aspect ratio is the relationship between width and height. A photo that's 1600×1200 has a 4:3 ratio. If you force it to 1600×600 you've changed the ratio, and everything in the picture looks stretched and distorted. The fix is to lock the aspect ratio: set one dimension and let the tool calculate the other automatically. Only unlock it when you genuinely want to crop or distort on purpose.

You can shrink freely, but upscaling has limits

Making an image smaller is safe — you're discarding pixels you don't need, and the result looks clean. Making it bigger is the tricky direction. There's no hidden detail to recover, so the tool has to invent pixels by interpolating between existing ones. Modest upscaling (say 25%) is usually fine; doubling or tripling a small image leaves it soft and blocky. When in doubt, start from the highest-resolution original you have.

Frequently asked questions

Will resizing reduce the image quality?

Shrinking an image looks great. Enlarging beyond the original resolution softens detail, because the extra pixels have to be invented. For best results, downsize from a larger original rather than upscaling a small one.

Are my photos uploaded to a server?

No. The resizing happens entirely in your browser, so your image never leaves your device. There's no account and nothing stored.

How do I resize to an exact file size, like under 2 MB?

File size depends on dimensions and compression together. Resize to smaller dimensions first, then lower the quality if needed — see the reduce file size for email guide for a step-by-step approach.
