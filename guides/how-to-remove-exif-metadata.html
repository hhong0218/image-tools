How to Remove EXIF Data From Photos

Every photo you take quietly records hidden information about it — including, very often, the exact GPS coordinates where it was shot. Before you post that picture publicly, here's why you should strip that data, and how to do it in seconds.

What is EXIF data?

EXIF (Exchangeable Image File Format) is metadata your camera or phone embeds inside every JPG and HEIC photo. It's invisible when you look at the picture, but it's right there in the file. Typical EXIF fields include:

  • GPS coordinates — the precise latitude and longitude where the photo was taken.
  • Date and time — down to the second.
  • Device info — camera or phone make and model.
  • Camera settings — exposure, aperture, ISO, focal length, whether the flash fired.
  • Software — what edited the photo, sometimes including your name or device account.

Why EXIF is a real privacy risk

The dangerous one is location. A photo taken in your living room and posted online can carry the GPS coordinates of your home. Pictures of children, a "for sale" item shot in your driveway, or a casual selfie can all reveal exactly where you live, work, or spend time. There have been documented cases of people being located through the GPS tags in photos they shared publicly.

It's worth knowing that major social networks usually strip EXIF on upload — but not all of them, not always, and certainly not when you email a file, send it over a messaging app, post it to a forum, or attach it to a marketplace listing. The only safe assumption is that the metadata travels with the file unless you remove it yourself.

Frequently asked questions

Does removing EXIF change how the photo looks?

No. EXIF is invisible metadata; stripping it leaves the visible image unchanged (aside from one extra compression pass on re-export).

Do social media sites already remove EXIF?

Many do on upload, but behavior varies and email, messaging, forums, and file shares typically keep it. Strip it yourself to be sure.

Is the photo uploaded to remove the data?

No. Our tool processes the image entirely in your browser, so the file — and its location data — never leaves your device.
