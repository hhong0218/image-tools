How to Convert HEIC to JPG

Your iPhone saved a photo as .HEIC and now Windows, a website, or an old app refuses to open it. Here's how to turn it into a universal JPG in seconds — free, with nothing uploaded to a server.

Why iPhones use HEIC (and why it's a problem)

Since iOS 11, Apple devices have saved photos as HEIC (High Efficiency Image Container). The format stores a photo at roughly half the size of an equivalent JPG, which means you fit twice as many pictures on your phone at the same visual quality. That efficiency is genuinely useful — right up until you try to use the file somewhere outside Apple's ecosystem.

Windows Photo Viewer, many web upload forms, older image editors, and plenty of messaging tools simply can't read HEIC. The fix is to convert the file to JPG, the most widely-supported image format in existence. Almost everything made in the last 25 years opens a JPG without complaint.

The fastest way: convert in your browser

You don't need to install an app or send your photos to a stranger's server. Our HEIC to JPG converter decodes the file locally using the open-source libheif engine (compiled to WebAssembly), so the photo never leaves your device.

  1. Open the HEIC to JPG converter.
  2. Drag your .heic photo onto the drop zone, or click to browse. On Windows you may need to type *.heic or choose "All files" in the picker, because the file dialog sometimes hides HEIC by default.
  3. Pick a JPG quality. 92% keeps the photo crisp while still shrinking the file; drop lower only if you need a smaller result.
  4. Click Convert to JPG. A large photo takes a couple of seconds to decode locally.
  5. Check the preview, then hit Download JPG. Done.

Will converting lose quality?

JPG is a lossy format, so technically there's always some compression. In practice, at 90% quality or higher the difference is invisible to the eye for ordinary photos. If a picture really matters — a print, a portfolio shot — keep the quality slider high. If you just need to email or upload it, a slightly lower setting trims the file size without anyone noticing.

One thing to remember: converting HEIC to JPG is a one-way trip in terms of efficiency. The JPG will usually be larger on disk than the HEIC it came from, because JPG is an older, less space-efficient format. That's a fair trade for being able to open the file anywhere.

What about EXIF data and location?

HEIC photos carry metadata — the date, camera settings, and often GPS coordinates of where the photo was taken. If you plan to share the JPG publicly, consider stripping that data first with our EXIF remover so you're not accidentally publishing your home address.
