How to Compress Images Without Losing Quality

"Compress without losing quality" is a slightly impossible promise — but you can get remarkably close. With the right format and settings, you can cut an image's file size by 60–80% with no visible difference. Here's how it actually works.

Why file size and quality are linked

Photographs contain an enormous amount of detail, most of which your eye can't fully distinguish. Lossy compression (used by JPG and WebP) takes advantage of that: it throws away the information you're least likely to notice and keeps what matters. The art is in throwing away just enough.

That's why "without losing quality" really means "without losing visible quality." Below a certain point you start to see blocky artifacts and muddy colors; above it, the savings keep coming for free. Your job is to find that sweet spot — and for most photos it sits around 75–85% quality.

The three levers that control file size

1. Quality setting

This is the biggest lever for photos. Exporting a JPG at 100% versus 80% can halve the file size, and most people can't tell the two apart. Use the live preview in our tools to nudge the quality down until you just start to notice softening, then bump it back up one notch.

2. Format

The format you save in matters as much as the quality slider. WebP typically produces files 25–35% smaller than JPG at the same visible quality, and it's supported by every modern browser. For photos, switching JPG to WebP is often the single easiest win. (For a full breakdown, see WebP vs PNG vs JPG.)

3. Dimensions

This is the lever people forget. A 6000×4000 photo from a phone has 24 million pixels. If it's only ever displayed at 1200px wide on a website, you're storing — and forcing visitors to download — about 20× more data than you need. Resizing the image down to the size it will actually be shown is the most "lossless-feeling" compression there is, because the discarded pixels were never visible anyway.

A practical workflow

  1. Resize first. Scale the image down to the largest size it will actually be displayed at. This alone often does most of the work.
  2. Choose WebP if your destination supports it (almost every website does), otherwise JPG.
  3. Start at 80% quality and watch the preview. Lower it until you see degradation, then go back up one step.
  4. Compare file sizes. If WebP and JPG come out close, pick WebP for the smaller download and better browser caching.

When you genuinely can't lose anything

For logos, screenshots with text, line art, or anything with sharp edges, lossy compression looks terrible — it smears the crisp boundaries. For those, use PNG, which is lossless. PNG files are bigger, but the image is mathematically identical to the original. If you need transparency and small size together, WebP (in its lossless mode) is a good middle ground.

Frequently asked questions

How much can I shrink a photo safely?

For a typical phone photo, expect to cut 60–80% of the file size with no visible loss by combining a sensible resize with 80% WebP/JPG quality.

Is repeatedly compressing a JPG bad?

Yes — every lossy save discards a little more detail (generation loss). Always compress from the original file, not from a file you already compressed.

Which is smaller, JPG or WebP?

WebP is almost always smaller than JPG at the same visible quality, usually by 25–35%.
